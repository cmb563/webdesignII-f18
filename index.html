Benny Goodman

The King of Swing

About

His passion was music and his big band sound aquickened the pulse of a generation ready to shrug off the Depression and dance. With clarinet in hand, Benny Goodm an was transformed from a child in Chicago’s impoverished Jewish ghetto into the king of swing, greeted with near pandemonium wherever his band played. Goodman led jazz into the commercial mainstream.

That brought with him an extraordinary group of gifted and original musicians. Band members Teddy Wilson and Lionel Hampton were some of the first to break the big band color barrier. Held together by the force of Goodman’s personality and a willingness to put their music above all else, the Benny Goodman Band created a kingdom of swing for all.

Legacy

Despite his failing health, Goodman continued to perform during the 1980s. He died of heart failure on June 13, 1986, in New York City—just days after his final performance. Not long before his death, he had received a Lifetime Achievement Grammy Award as well as honorary degrees from Brandeis University and Bard College.

Still remembered as one of jazz’s greatest artists, Goodman was featured on a postage stamp in 1996 as part of the Legends of American Music series.

The Band



Harry James

Harry James was one of the most outstanding artist to work with instuments durning the swing era, employing a bravura playing style that made his trumpet work instantly identifiable. Harry James had worked along the side of Benny for several of years as well as working individually.

Manny Klein

One of the busiest trumpeters of the 1930s, Manny Klein appeared on a countless number of recordings through the decades. Manny's talent was playing the saxophone in smaller and sometimes larger bands. He had joined Benny Goodman and his band when they were starting to tour.

Babe Russin

If a contest was held to see what tenor saxophonist had appeared on the most records and played on the most gigs, the winner would no doubt be someone like Babe Russin. Benny and Babe became great friends overtime while working on so much together.

Ernani Bernardi

Bernardi was a musician in the big band era, playing lead sax for some of the greats. He is best known for his arrangements of two of the mid-20th century’s most popular dance melodies. He was the lead alto sax for several of big bands of the era such as those of Benny Goodman.
